\begin{abstract}
Small language models are increasingly promoted as low-cost, private, and edge-deployable alternatives to cloud-scale LLMs. Yet most evaluations assume favorable conditions: expert-written prompts, clean benchmark inputs, and high-precision inference. This overlooks the lower-bound regime in which small models are often used by non-expert users and deployed under quantized or compressed settings. We introduce \textbf{PEEL} (\textbf{P}rompt--\textbf{E}dge \textbf{E}valuation \textbf{L}adder), a two-axis protocol for evaluating small language models as prompt quality and deployment quality degrade. PEEL varies prompts from expert-formatted instructions to colloquial and broken inputs, and varies deployment from full precision to low-bit quantization. We evaluate 16 small and efficient models across reading comprehension, open-domain question answering, truthfulness, instruction following, and multi-turn dialogue. Our results show that prompt degradation is often more damaging than quantization. After controlling for output-scoring artefacts, TruthfulQA accuracy changes by only about one point from full precision to 4-bit inference under expert prompts. In contrast, SQuAD~v2 exact match falls from roughly 30\% under expert prompts to 2--5\% under broken prompts across all models and deployment settings. We also find that prompt robustness is strongly shaped by post-training quality: at matched 1.5B scale, the strongest instruction-tuned hybrid model scores 8.20 on MT-Bench, compared with 4.06 for a same-size Transformer. Finally, safety alignment remains fragile, with models refusing only 6.5\% of unsafe prompts on average. PEEL exposes the gap between clean benchmark performance and ordinary-user edge reliability, and provides a practical protocol for lower-bound evaluation.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Small language models (SLMs) are increasingly positioned as practical alternatives to cloud-scale large language models (LLMs). With fewer parameters and lower memory requirements, they can run on consumer laptops, mobile devices, and other resource-constrained hardware, reducing dependence on cloud APIs for latency-sensitive, low-cost, and privacy-sensitive applications. This makes SLMs attractive not only as smaller models, but also as a possible mechanism for broadening access to language technologies under realistic hardware, connectivity, and privacy constraints.

However, this accessibility promise remains only partially tested. Most evaluations measure small models under favorable conditions: expert-written prompts, clean benchmark inputs, and high-precision inference on well-supported infrastructure. These settings estimate an upper bound on model capability, but they do not reflect many real uses of local or edge-deployed models. Non-expert users may provide vague, colloquial, underspecified, or malformed prompts. At the same time, edge deployment often relies on compressed or quantized models because of limited memory, cost, energy, or connectivity. A model that performs well with expert prompts under full-precision inference may fail once it is both queried informally and deployed under constrained runtime conditions.

We argue that SLM evaluation should therefore report not only upper-bound benchmark performance, but also \emph{lower-bound reliability}: how models behave when user input quality and deployment quality degrade together. This regime is especially important because the users and settings most likely to benefit from SLMs are also those least represented by idealized evaluation protocols.

Existing evaluations cover parts of this space, but rarely their interaction. General LLM benchmarks measure broad capability under standardized prompts and stable inference settings. Edge and on-device benchmarks move evaluation closer to resource-constrained deployment, but typically hold prompting conditions fixed. Conversely, prompt-robustness studies perturb prompt quality while leaving the serving stack largely unchanged. As a result, we have limited evidence about the joint effect of non-expert prompting and edge deployment constraints---precisely the regime occupied by ordinary users running small models locally.

We close this gap with \textbf{PEEL} (\textbf{P}rompt--\textbf{E}dge \textbf{E}valuation \textbf{L}adder), a two-axis evaluation protocol for lower-bound assessment of small language models. PEEL crosses four prompt-quality tiers, from expert task instructions to broken ordinary-user prompts, with five edge-deployment settings, from full-precision GPU inference to aggressive low-bit local inference. Across this ladder, PEEL evaluates task accuracy, hallucination discrimination, safety refusal, multi-turn instruction-following quality, latency, and throughput. PEEL asks a deployment-facing question: what remains reliable when both the user and the serving stack are imperfect?

We evaluate 16 sub-4B models across eight model families, covering extractive reading comprehension, open-domain question answering, truthfulness, hallucination discrimination, safety behavior, and multi-turn instruction following. Our central finding is that small models do not simply become weaker under edge constraints; they become more conditional. Reliability depends sharply on task format, prompt quality, chat-template fidelity, and the scoring assumptions used to interpret outputs. Across models and deployment settings, prompt degradation is often more damaging than bit-width reduction. On SQuAD~v2, exact match falls from roughly 30\% under expert prompts to \textbf{2--5\%} under broken prompts across every edge setting, indicating a near-total prompt-driven collapse in span-based reading comprehension. In contrast, after controlling for output-scoring artefacts, TruthfulQA-MC1 changes by only about one point from full precision to 4-bit inference under expert prompts. These results suggest that, for many practical SLM deployments, the main reliability bottleneck is not quantization alone, but the interaction between task format, user prompt quality, and runtime assumptions.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Lower-bound framing.} We formulate ordinary-user edge deployment as a lower-bound reliability problem, where prompt and deployment quality degrade jointly.
\item \textbf{PEEL protocol and benchmark.} We introduce a two-axis protocol that crosses prompt-quality tiers with edge-deployment settings, and evaluate 16 sub-4B models across QA, truthfulness, hallucination, safety, and dialogue tasks.
\item \textbf{Prompt--edge reliability analysis.} We show that prompt degradation can dominate bit-width reduction for format-sensitive tasks, while multiple-choice truthfulness is relatively stable once scoring artefacts are handled.
\item \textbf{Reproducible artifacts.} We release PEEL prompts, deployment configurations, scoring scripts, and result grids for future lower-bound evaluation of edge-deployed SLMs.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Small language models and edge deployment.}
Large language models have achieved strong performance across reasoning, question answering, and instruction-following tasks, but their memory, compute, and energy costs remain substantial for local or latency-sensitive deployment. This has motivated growing interest in compact and efficient model families, including TinyLlama~\cite{TinyLlama}, Phi~\cite{phi4}, Gemma~\cite{gemma2}, Qwen~\cite{qwen2025}, Llama-3.2~\cite{llama3}, and Falcon-H1~\cite{falconh1}. These models aim to preserve useful language capabilities while reducing inference cost.


Recently, small language models (SLMs) have shown great potential in alleviating these issues
while achieving performance comparable to LLMs for domain-specific problems [1~\cite{phi}, 27~\cite{bellagente2024stablelm216b}, 118~\cite{OLMo}, 146~\cite{mobilellm}, 227,
281, 339~\cite{gemma2}, 342~\cite{MobiLlama}, 359~\cite{slmsurvey}, 407\cite{qwen2025}, 444~\cite{TinyLlama}]. 


A parallel line of work studies edge and on-device LLM deployment, motivated by latency, privacy, bandwidth, and cost constraints~\cite{llm_edge,smlbenchmark,Mobileaibench,li2025palmbench}. Existing studies have explored model compression, efficient inference engines, split execution, and hardware-aware optimization for mobile and edge devices. This work makes local LLM deployment increasingly feasible, but typically focuses on efficiency and deployability rather than user-facing reliability under imperfect interaction conditions.



\paragraph{Benchmarking LLMs and edge LLMs.}
General-purpose LLM benchmarks evaluate broad language capabilities, including knowledge, reasoning, question answering, instruction following, and dialogue quality. Widely used benchmarks and evaluation suites include MMLU~\cite{mmlu2021}, HELM~\cite{helm2022}, SQuAD~v2~\cite{squad2}, Natural Questions~\cite{naturalquestions}, TruthfulQA~\cite{truthfulqa}, HaluEval~\cite{halueval}, and MT-Bench~\cite{mtbench2023}. Recent SLM-oriented evaluation work, such as JudgeBoard~\cite{smlbenchmark}, further improves standardized assessment of small-model reasoning and generation quality.

Recent mobile and edge benchmarks, including MobileAIBench~\cite{Mobileaibench}, PalmBench~\cite{li2025palmbench}, MELT~\cite{melt}, and related edge-LLM benchmarking efforts, move evaluation closer to resource-constrained deployment by measuring latency, throughput, memory use, energy consumption, and device compatibility. However, general benchmarks typically assume clean prompts and stable inference infrastructure, while edge benchmarks often hold prompting protocols fixed.

\paragraph{Quantization and local inference.}
Post-training quantization reduces the memory and computation required for LLM inference by representing model weights, and sometimes activations, at lower precision. Methods such as GPTQ~\cite{gptq2022}, LLM.int8()~\cite{llmint8}, AWQ~\cite{awq}, and group-wise quantization~\cite{groupwisequant} show that LLMs can often be compressed to 8-bit, 4-bit, or lower precision with limited average benchmark degradation. Lightweight inference engines and deployment frameworks such as \texttt{llama.cpp}/GGUF~\cite{gguf} and MLC-LLM~\cite{mlc} further enable quantized models to run on CPUs, mobile devices, and heterogeneous edge hardware.

These systems are central to practical SLM deployment, but most quantization studies evaluate accuracy under fixed, expert-style prompts. They therefore provide strong evidence about how bit-width affects model outputs when prompting is controlled, but less evidence about how quantization interacts with non-expert prompting, chat-template mismatch, and output-scoring assumptions.


\paragraph{Prompt robustness, hallucination, and safety.}
Language models are highly sensitive to prompt wording, formatting, and task specification. Prompt-robustness benchmarks such as PromptBench~\cite{zhu2023promptrobust} and RobustAlpacaEval~\cite{ralpaca2024} show that prompt perturbations can substantially affect model performance, and that worst-case prompt performance can provide a meaningful lower bound on reliability. This is especially relevant to non-expert users, who may not know how to write precise task instructions or preserve benchmark-specific answer formats.

Trustworthiness is also central to user-facing SLM deployment. Prior work has studied truthfulness, hallucination, toxicity, and unsafe instruction following through benchmarks such as TruthfulQA~\cite{truthfulqa}, HaluEval~\cite{halueval}, and safety-oriented refusal tests. These issues are particularly important for small local models, which may have weaker instruction tuning and safety alignment while also bypassing cloud-side safety layers.

Prior work has separately advanced small-model deployment, quantized local inference, standardized capability evaluation, edge benchmarking, prompt robustness, and trustworthiness assessment. However, these lines of work usually vary one dimension at a time: deployment studies often fix prompts, while prompt-robustness studies often fix the serving stack. PEEL connects these dimensions by jointly varying prompt quality and deployment quality, measuring how task accuracy, hallucination discrimination, safety refusal, dialogue quality, latency, and throughput change under ordinary-user edge conditions.


\section{Benchmark Design}

Typically, LMs that exhibit emergent abilities are classified as LLMs. However, the categorization
of SLMs remains unclear. Studies vary in their contexts: some define SLMs as models with fewer
than one billion parameters [227], while others consider the term “small language model” relative
to the larger counterparts [186, 333, 375], with no consensus on a unified definition in the current
landscape of LLMs. Research suggests SLMs for mobile devices, typically possessing around 6 GB
of memory, consist of sub-billion parameter models [227], whereas others classify models with up
to 10 billion parameters as small, noting their lack of emergent abilities [107]. Given their use in
resource-constrained environments and for specific tasks, we propose a generalized definition:
Given specific tasks and resource constraints, we define SLMs as falling within a range where the lower
bound is the minimum size at which the model exhibits emergent abilities for a specialized task, and
the upper bound is the largest size manageable within limited resource conditions. This definition

\subsection{The 2D Degradation Space}

PEEL evaluates every combination of a five-level \emph{edge deployment
axis} and a four-level \emph{prompt quality axis}, forming a 4$\times$5 grid.
Figure~\ref{fig:collapse} shows a populated instance; Appendix
Figure~\ref{fig:space} gives a schematic overview.

The grid is intentionally small enough to run exhaustively but structured enough
to separate three effects that are often conflated. First, E0$\to$E1 isolates
GPU-side precision reduction while retaining the HuggingFace inference stack.
Second, E1$\to$E2 crosses from the HuggingFace/chat-template path into a
CPU-oriented GGUF runtime. Third, E2$\to$E4 varies local quantization strength
inside the same runtime family. On the prompt axis, P0$\to$P3 progressively
removes task scaffolding. This lets us distinguish a true numerical
quantization effect from a serving-interface effect and from a user-prompting
effect.

\paragraph{Edge Deployment Settings (E0--E4).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{E0-REF}: BF16, HuggingFace \texttt{transformers}, GPU.
  \item \textbf{E1-INT8}: 8-bit static quantization via \texttt{bitsandbytes}~\cite{llmint8}, GPU.
  \item \textbf{E2-Q8}: Q8\_0 GGUF via \texttt{llama.cpp}, CPU.
  \item \textbf{E3-Q5}: Q5\_K\_M GGUF via \texttt{llama.cpp}, CPU.
  \item \textbf{E4-Q4}: Q4\_K\_M GGUF via \texttt{llama.cpp}, CPU.
\end{itemize}
E2--E4 use CPU-only inference to simulate consumer edge hardware with no
discrete GPU.

We treat these settings as \emph{deployment conditions}, not only as bit widths.
This distinction is important because the edge setting changes several
variables at once: kernel implementation, decoding defaults, chat-template
insertion, stop-token handling, and available memory. Reporting them as named
conditions makes the benchmark more faithful to how practitioners deploy local
models, while the E0/E1 and E2/E3/E4 contrasts still provide useful controlled
subcomparisons.

\paragraph{Prompt Quality Tiers (P0--P3).}
We define four tiers aligned with realistic user archetypes drawn from
AI-literacy and human-computer interaction literature~\cite{sclar2023,multiprompt2024}.
The tiers span the spectrum from expert benchmark-style prompting to the informal
interaction style documented in studies of first-time LLM users:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{P0 -- Expert}: Structured, instruction-following prompt
    identical to standard benchmark format. Explicit task description, labelled
    fields, output format constraint.
  \item \textbf{P1 -- Casual}: Simplified but grammatically correct. Removes
    technical terminology and output format instructions.
  \item \textbf{P2 -- Naive}: Minimal content only; no task framing; relies on
    the model to infer the task type from context alone.
  \item \textbf{P3 -- Broken}: Colloquial, abbreviated, syntactically informal.
    Typical of a first-time LLM user with no prompting background.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Prompt-tier internal validity.}
Each tier is defined by a \emph{functional specification} (what information
is withheld and how grammar is degraded) rather than a single template, allowing
future work to instantiate the taxonomy with alternative phrasings.
MT-Bench P0--P3 deltas vary systematically by family
(Section~\ref{sec:arch}), supporting P1--P3 as a diagnostic stress test rather
than a calibrated user-study scale.
We also ground the tiers in real usage. We sampled 50 single-turn English
prompts from WildChat-1M~\cite{wildchat2024} and had two fluent annotators
independently map each to the nearest PEEL tier. A preliminary non-native pilot
pass was used only to refine the rubric and is excluded from the agreement
statistics. The two retained annotators agree at Cohen
$\kappa=0.51$ (70\% raw; moderate). Crucially, \textbf{80--88\% of real prompts
fall below the expert tier}---the majority at P1, and only 6--10 of 50 at
P0---confirming that non-expert users predominantly write the casual,
under-specified prompts that P1--P3 are designed to model. A larger, calibrated
user study remains future work, but this grounding indicates the degraded tiers
reflect real behaviour rather than artificial adversarial edits.

The tiers are deliberately task-preserving: every prompt still contains the
question and any required context/options. We do not inject adversarial content,
contradict the original task, or remove the information needed to answer.
Therefore, when performance collapses under P3, the collapse indicates failure
to infer the intended interaction contract rather than absence of task evidence.
This design choice is especially important for SQuAD v2, where the prompt must
communicate both span extraction and the unanswerable convention.

\subsection{Datasets}

We select tasks that expose different output contracts. SQuAD v2 requires
extractive spans and a special unanswerable marker; NQ-Open requires factual
recall without context; TruthfulQA-MC1 requires selecting among explicit
options; HaluEval requires binary judgment; and MT-Bench~\cite{mtbench2023}
evaluates open-ended
multi-turn instruction following. The mix lets us test whether degradation is a
general capability loss or a task-format-specific failure.

\paragraph{Reading Comprehension -- SQuAD v2~\cite{squad2}.}
Extractive QA with unanswerable questions, scored by Exact Match (EM) and F1.
Unanswerable questions require outputting the literal string
\textit{``unanswerable''}, making format adherence part of the evaluation signal.

\paragraph{Open-Domain QA -- Natural Questions Open~\cite{nqopen}.}
Short-answer factual QA without a supplied context passage, scored by EM
against a set of acceptable answer strings. Tests parametric knowledge retention
under prompt simplification.

\paragraph{Truthfulness Discrimination -- TruthfulQA-MC1~\cite{truthfulqa}.}
Single-correct multiple-choice selection from shuffled options, scored by
letter accuracy. We use a generative MC1 protocol (model selects a letter)
rather than log-likelihood scoring, making evaluation fully model-agnostic and
compatible with all runners.

HaluEval~\cite{halueval2023} is included at P0 for hallucination detection
baselines; the judge-style instruction is intrinsic to its prompt and is not
tier-degradable. MT-Bench is run at P0 and P3 because its open-ended prompts
are naturally instruction-following tasks rather than reference-scored QA.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

Reference-scored tasks use fully offline metrics. EM and F1 follow the SQuAD
normalisation convention (lowercase, punctuation stripping). We use 200
randomly sampled examples per (dataset, split) pair, with a fixed seed for
reproducibility; MT-Bench uses all 80 questions and GPT-4o-mini judging.
Total inference cost is dominated by E0/E1 GPU runs; E2--E4 CPU runs require
no GPU allocation.

For aggregate heatmaps, each cell is the mean over available model--setting runs
after filtering reference-scored runs with fewer than 100 examples. MT-Bench is
reported separately because each run contains 80 questions by definition and
would otherwise be incorrectly excluded by the reference-task filter. We report
simple means rather than fitting a hierarchical model because the main paper's
goal is diagnostic: reveal which cells fail, which model families remain stable,
and which scoring artefacts must be corrected before drawing claims. Appendix
tables provide the coverage counts needed to interpret missing or partially
covered cells.

\paragraph{Statistical reporting.}
For headline comparisons we report 95\% confidence intervals from a
nonparametric bootstrap over \emph{models} (10{,}000 resamples), which captures
the large between-model heterogeneity here (median per-cell half-width
${\pm}2.4$\,pp). Non-overlapping intervals therefore indicate an effect visible
across the model population---a deliberately conservative test given the wide
between-model variance.

We also audit every result before including it in the claims matrix. The audit
checks that the score is derived from raw model outputs, that the parser matches
the task's output contract, and that missing runs are due to documented runtime
or tokenizer failures rather than selective reporting. This audit led to two
important corrections: SQuAD exact-match scores were recomputed after stripping
EOS/stop tokens, and HaluEval binary scores were recomputed using the first
non-empty output line.

\subsection{Model Selection}

We evaluate 16 models spanning eight brand families (Falcon-H1,
Gemma, InternLM, Llama, Phi, Qwen, SmolLM, TinyLlama) across the
0.09B--3.8B parameter range (Table~\ref{tab:models}).

We prioritize the 0.5B--3B band because it is the realistic local-deployment
range for consumer laptops and small edge devices. Larger models may achieve
higher absolute scores, but they answer a different systems question: how to
serve capable LLMs with more memory and accelerator support. PEEL instead
focuses on the regime where memory pressure makes quantization necessary and
where users are likely to run models through lightweight local runtimes.

\subsection{Deployment Configurations}

GPU settings (E0, E1) run on NVIDIA A100 SXM4 (40\,GB) accelerators on an
institutional HPC cluster.
CPU settings (E2--E4) use \texttt{llama.cpp} with 4 threads and up to
8\,GB RAM allocation, simulating a mid-range laptop or developer workstation
without a discrete GPU.
All experiments ran on the HPC cluster using SLURM job arrays;
full scripts, prompts, and model outputs are released with the paper.

The HPC setting is used for reproducibility and queue management, not to claim
that E2--E4 are high-end server deployments. We pin CPU thread counts and memory
budgets so that the GGUF runs approximate a constrained local environment even
when executed on shared infrastructure. To check that this abstraction is not
misleading, we additionally ran a consumer-workstation sanity check for selected
Falcon-H1 and Qwen models (Appendix~\ref{app:details}). We use it only to
confirm that CPU-only GGUF inference is practical on consumer hardware; the main
numerical claims use the reproducible HPC runs.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{TruthfulQA-MC1: Quantization-Resilient}
\label{sec:tqa}

Across all 1,088 qualifying runs (16 models, num\_examples $\geq$ 100),
TruthfulQA-MC1 mean accuracy at the expert baseline (P0, E0-REF) is 39.7\%;
it is 39.4\% at E1-INT8 and settles at 38.4--40.1\% at E2--E4. All cells remain
well above the 25.0\% chance baseline. Crucially, quantization from E0 to E4
changes P0 accuracy by only $-$1.1\,pp---E0-REF 39.7\% (95\% CI [30.9, 48.9])
and E4-Q4 38.6\% [31.0, 46.8] have heavily overlapping intervals---and the
P0$\to$P3 drop within any single setting stays under 12\,pp: a \emph{near-zero
interaction} between the two degradation axes for this task type.

One notable pattern (Table~\ref{tab:tqa_grid}): at E2--E4 (CPU/llama.cpp),
the P1 ``casual'' prompt tier scores \emph{lower} than at E0/E1
(29.1--31.6\% vs.\ 40.6--41.0\%), while P0 is unaffected.
We attribute this to llama.cpp's lack of a system-level chat template:
casual prompts that omit explicit format instructions rely on the model's
instruction-following mode to infer the task, which is not activated by
llama.cpp's plain-text interface.

This pattern is useful because it separates two hypotheses. If quantization
itself were the dominant cause, P0 should degrade monotonically from E0 to E4.
Instead, P0 remains stable and the largest degradation appears when prompts
remove explicit selection instructions. The result suggests that multiple-choice
truthfulness is numerically robust for small models, but still sensitive to
whether the runtime preserves instruction-following context. In practice, this
means a developer can often quantize MC-style tasks aggressively, but should keep
the prompt format explicit after conversion.

\subsection{SQuAD v2: Prompt-Driven Collapse}
\label{sec:collapse}

After correcting for an EOS/stop-token scoring artefact (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}),
SQuAD v2 EM at P0 is stable at 27.8--30.5\% across all five edge settings,
with no significant capability cliff at the E1$\to$E2 (\texttt{llama.cpp})
boundary (full grid in appendix Table~\ref{tab:squad_grid};
the heatmap in Figure~\ref{fig:collapse} visualises the same data).
The \emph{dominant failure mode is prompt degradation}, not quantization:
EM falls to 2--5\% at P3 for \emph{all} models and settings---a near-total
collapse of roughly $10\times$ from P0. Pooled over settings, P0 EM is 29.6\%
(95\% CI [22.7, 36.5]) versus 3.2\% [2.8, 3.7] at P3; the non-overlapping
intervals make prompt degradation the statistically unambiguous driver. The
degradation is monotonic but not uniform: P1 still retains 17--21\%, whereas P2
and P3 drop to 3--6\% and 2--5\%, so the cliff lies between P1 and P2.

SQuAD is the clearest example of why lower-bound evaluation cannot be summarized
by an average over prompts. Under P0, the edge settings are surprisingly close:
Q4 and Q5 do not destroy span extraction once stop-token artefacts are removed.
Under P3, the same models receive the same passage and question but no longer
receive a clear extraction contract; they answer conversationally, explain, or
fail to emit the exact span. Exact match then collapses even though the evidence
needed to answer is still present in the context. This is not a generic
``small models are bad'' result. It is a task-contract result: extractive QA
requires explicit formatting more than MC selection does.

\subsection{NQ-Open: Recall Halves at the GPU$\to$CPU Boundary}
\label{sec:nq}

Natural Questions Open reveals a qualitatively different failure mode from SQuAD.
At E0-REF, mean P0 EM is 8.2\% (8.4\% at E1-INT8), with the strongest models
(Phi-4-Mini, Llama-3.2-3B, Falcon-H1-1.5B-Deep) reaching 12--18\%. At E2--E4
(llama.cpp CPU), P0 EM roughly \emph{halves} to \textbf{3.3--3.6\%} across all
prompt tiers (P0 8.3\% [5.8, 10.8] at E0/E1 vs.\ 3.5\% [2.4, 4.8] at E2--E4,
non-overlapping; full grid in appendix Table~\ref{tab:nq_grid}).

The E1$\to$E2 drop is caused by two compounding factors:
(1)~\texttt{llama.cpp} does not apply the model's chat template, so models
run in raw-completion mode---NQ-Open requires answer recall without a supporting
passage, which demands active instruction-following; and
(2)~sub-2B models have limited parametric factual recall, which is roughly
halved once instruction-following mode is lost.
This finding highlights a key distinction between
knowledge-intensive open-domain QA and reading comprehension:
quantization matters for the former even under expert prompting.

The NQ result should be read conservatively: exact match is harsh and the modest
E0/E1 scores ($\sim$8\%) show these models store limited factual knowledge without
retrieval. Still, the halving to $\sim$3.5\% at E2--E4 marks a deployment boundary
where much of that recall is lost, so small local models are weak standalone
factual QA systems without retrieval augmentation.

\subsection{HaluEval: Binary Classification and Parser Artefacts}
\label{sec:halueval}

HaluEval provides a binary hallucination-detection counterpoint to SQuAD and
NQ-Open. Under the corrected first-line parser, HaluEval-General decreases
from 52.2\% at E0-REF to 43.5\% at E4-Q4, while HaluEval-QA stays in the
38--47\% range across settings (Table~\ref{tab:halueval_grid}). These results
show degradation, but not the near-total P3-style collapse observed for SQuAD.

prompt embeds the label words can turn a usable answer into an ambiguous label.
We therefore treat parser specification as part of the benchmark protocol.

\subsection{MT-Bench: Instruction Following Under Degradation}
\label{sec:mtbench}

We evaluate MT-Bench (80 multi-turn questions, 8 categories) with GPT-4o-mini
as judge at all five edge settings and two prompt tiers (P0, P3), totalling
156 scored runs. Coverage is not perfectly rectangular: InternLM and Phi have
partial setting coverage due to tokenizer and chat-template failures.

Setting-level means (appendix Table~\ref{tab:app_mtbench_setting})

E2+ decline is concentrated in families whose chat template \texttt{llama.cpp}

effect: the P0$\to$P3 drop is at most 0.21 points at any single setting.

missing chat-template handling---rather than to Falcon-H1-specific architecture.

This MT-Bench pattern contrasts with SQuAD. Open-ended instruction following is
less dependent on exact output form, so informal prompts usually reduce score
gradually rather than catastrophically. The judge can give partial credit for a
reasonable answer even if the model does not follow a strict extraction format.
Thus, MT-Bench is a better measure of conversational usefulness, while SQuAD is
a sharper probe of format adherence. Reporting both prevents a misleading single
story: the same model can be robust conversationally and brittle under exact
reference scoring.

\subsection{Architecture Family Comparison on MT-Bench}
\label{sec:arch}

At E0-REF/P0, several families reach the top band: Phi-3.5 and Falcon-H1-1.5B
both score ${\sim}$8.2, with Gemma-3-1B (family mean 7.40) and Phi-4 close
behind, while Qwen, SmolLM, and TinyLlama sit below 4. We therefore do
\emph{not} claim Falcon-H1 is strongest overall. Our sharpest architecture
result is a \emph{matched-scale} one: at 1.5B parameters, Falcon-H1-1.5B scores
\textbf{8.20} versus Qwen2.5-1.5B at \textbf{4.06} (a 4.14-point gap), isolating
architecture from parameter count.

Prompt degradation barely moves judged quality for the stronger families. From
P0 to P3, Falcon-H1 loses only $\mathbf{0.11}$ points as a family average (the
flagship Falcon-H1-1.5B is flat, 8.08$\to$8.20 over settings), and Gemma,
InternLM, and Phi are similarly stable ($|\Delta|\le0.11$). The clearest
degradation is among weaker Transformer baselines: Qwen2.5-1.5B drops from
4.17 to 3.60 ($-$0.57) and Llama by 0.30. Prompt robustness therefore tracks
\emph{instruction-following quality} more than a single architecture: Falcon-H1
is robust, but so are the strong dense Transformers (Gemma, Phi).

We avoid a mechanistic claim. The experiment does not ablate Falcon-H1's
internal modules, and because non-SSM families (Gemma, Phi) are equally
prompt-robust, the data do not support attributing robustness to the SSM
component. The defensible benchmark-level statement is narrower: at matched
scale and under identical tasks, judge, tiers, and settings, Falcon-H1 reaches
the top band and resists prompt degradation, whereas a same-size dense
Transformer (Qwen2.5-1.5B) does neither.

\subsection{Safety Alignment Under Edge Deployment}
\label{sec:safety}

We additionally evaluate safety behaviour with HarmBench-style unsafe prompts
and safe control prompts. The current keyword-based classifier assigns each
response to \textsc{Refuse}, \textsc{Comply}, or \textsc{Ambiguous}. Across
75 model--setting pairs, the mean refusal rate on unsafe prompts is only
\textbf{6.5\%} (95\% CI [4.8, 8.3]), with a maximum of \textbf{35.4\%} for any single
model--setting pair and several pairs refusing essentially \textbf{0\%} of
unsafe requests. In other words, small edge models comply with the large
majority of unsafe prompts, and no configuration refuses reliably. This
indicates that safety alignment at the sub-2B scale is fragile under edge
deployment.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{Figures/fig5_safety_heatmap.pdf}
  \caption{Safety refusal behaviour across model--setting pairs. Edge
    deployment does not induce a consistent safety trend: some models become
    more compliant under quantization, while others over-refuse benign prompts.}
  \label{fig:safety}
\end{figure}

The most concerning result is not a specific quantization effect but the
uniformly low refusal ceiling: even the safest observed configuration refuses
only about a third of unsafe prompts. Quantization does not move refusal in a
consistent direction---some models become more compliant under compression while
others become slightly more refusing---so edge conversion neither systematically
improves nor systematically degrades safety. Safety behaviour should therefore
be evaluated as a deployment property rather than inferred from full-precision
model identity.

This result also cautions against treating refusal rate alone as alignment
quality. A higher refusal rate may indicate safer behaviour on unsafe prompts,
but it may also indicate over-refusal on benign prompts; conversely, a low
refusal rate may reflect helpfulness on safe prompts or unsafe compliance. For
this reason, we also measure over-refusal on safe control prompts (Appendix) and
keep the classifier as a diagnostic tool rather than a final safety benchmark.
The two rates must be read jointly: the low unsafe-prompt refusal we observe is a
safety concern precisely because it is not accompanied by uniformly high
helpfulness on safe prompts. To verify the classifier itself, a stratified
120-response recheck with GPT-4o agrees with it on 78\% of labels
(Cohen $\kappa=0.78$, substantial), with GPT-4o finding marginally \emph{more}
refusals; the low-refusal finding is therefore not an artefact of keyword
matching.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What PEEL's lower bound means.}
PEEL should not be read as a replacement for upper-bound benchmarks. A
model that performs poorly under P3 may still be useful when embedded in a
product with a carefully engineered prompt, retrieval layer, and output parser.
Likewise, a model that is robust under PEEL may still fail on tasks outside
our English QA and instruction-following suites. The lower-bound interpretation
is more specific: if a model, runtime, and prompt tier combination fails here,
then a practitioner should not assume that standard benchmark scores will
transfer to a lightly engineered local deployment.

\paragraph{Task type and prompt degradation as primary moderators.}
Our corrected results show that \emph{prompt quality}, not
quantization level, is the dominant driver of SQuAD performance collapse.
SQuAD v2 EM is stable at $\sim$30\% across E0--E4 under expert prompts
(P0), but falls to 2--5\% at P3 for every architecture and quantization
setting. TruthfulQA-MC1 shows the opposite pattern: robust to both axes. At P0,
accuracy changes by about 1\,pp across E0--E4; the 5.5\,pp drop in the
all-tier setting average comes from the P1 casual tier losing its chat template
on CPU (Section~\ref{sec:tqa}), not from quantization itself.
This task-format interaction motivates separate lower-bound reporting rather
than a single scalar headline metric.

The implication is that benchmark design should preserve task format as a first
class factor. Multiple-choice selection, span extraction, binary judgment, and
open-ended conversation do not degrade in the same way. A single average score
would hide the most actionable lesson in our data: quantized models can remain
usable when the answer space is constrained, while exact-reference tasks become
fragile when the prompt no longer specifies the contract.

\paragraph{Two scoring artefacts and their lesson.}
Our audit identified two independent scoring bugs with a shared root cause:
\texttt{llama.cpp} models at E2--E4 emit verbose, multi-line outputs that append
EOS/stop-token strings and repeated prompt instructions after the actual answer.
For \emph{SQuAD~v2}, surviving stop tokens block exact match and produce a
spurious 0\% EM at E2$+$; truncating to the first non-empty line and stripping
EOS tokens recovers ${\sim}$28--30\% EM, consistent with E0-REF. For \emph{HaluEval}, the
echoed instruction contains both ``yes'' and ``no'', so a full-text parser marks
50--62\% of E2--E4 responses \emph{ambiguous}; first-line parsing recovers
accuracy from ${\sim}$24\% to ${\sim}$44--51\% (mechanics in
Appendix~\ref{app:details}). Both are the same pitfall at different output
levels: any \texttt{llama.cpp} evaluation that parses free text without
first-line truncation will systematically underreport span-extraction \emph{and}
binary-classification accuracy. More broadly, local runtimes expose raw text that
hosted chat APIs hide behind message-role formatting and stop-token logic, so
benchmarks reusing hosted-model parsers on local outputs may measure interface
mismatch rather than capability. Edge-LLM benchmarks should publish parsers with
raw outputs and run a parser audit before ranking models.

\paragraph{Practical recommendations.}
For \emph{practitioners}: prefer multiple-choice or classification outputs over
extractive spans when running \texttt{llama.cpp} Q4/Q5; favour Falcon-H1
SSM-Hybrid models when 1.5B-scale instruction following is the target, because
they strongly outperform the same-size Qwen baseline without extra memory cost;
and re-test safety refusal after deployment conversion, since quantization does not produce a
monotonic safety trend. For \emph{benchmark builders}: report prompt-tier matched
results rather than a single prompt-averaged mean, keep judge-scored MT-Bench
separate from reference-scored tasks, and document missing cells explicitly---in
an edge benchmark, tokenizer and chat-template failures are themselves part of
the deployment burden.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
All datasets are English-only. P1--P3 prompt templates are handcrafted; our
50-prompt WildChat grounding (Section~\ref{sec:design}) indicates they match real
usage, but a larger calibrated user study remains future work.
The E1$\to$E2 boundary confound remains the largest open ablation: the Phi
MT-Bench collapse at the GPU-to-CPU boundary and the SQuAD/NQ parser failures
both implicate chat-template/runtime handling, but a full GGUF-on-GPU ablation
within the HPC cluster remains future work.
Finally, the current safety analysis uses keyword-based refusal detection. It is
useful for detecting instability across model--setting pairs, but it should not
be treated as a substitute for human red teaming or policy-specific safety
evaluation.

\paragraph{Ethical considerations.}
Our safety analysis shows that sub-2B models, once quantized for edge use, refuse
only a small fraction of unsafe prompts. We report this as a cautionary finding,
not a recipe: it underscores that deploying small local models on open user input
without an added safety layer carries real risk. The evaluation uses existing
public safety prompts and creates no new harmful capability; we release the
refusal diagnostics precisely to encourage pre-deployment safety testing. Because
PEEL is a lower-bound stress test, its scores should inform deployment risk
assessment rather than be read as endorsements of any model for unsupervised use.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced PEEL, a benchmark for simultaneously probing LLM performance
along two real-world degradation axes: edge quantization and prompt quality.
Across 1,088 reference-scored runs and 156 MT-Bench
judge-scored runs, four principal findings emerge:
(1)~task type is the strongest determinant of robustness---MC tasks degrade
gracefully while extraction tasks collapse under informal prompts;
(2)~Falcon-H1-1.5B substantially outperforms same-size Transformers on
instruction-following (8.20 vs.\ 4.06 MT-Bench at 1.5B scale) and remains
stable from P0 to P3;
(3)~quantization-induced output artefacts can masquerade as capability collapse
unless scoring strips stop tokens and prompt echoes; and
(4)~safety alignment is fragile at sub-2B scale: models refuse only 6.5\% of
unsafe prompts on average (max 35.4\%) across model--setting pairs, and
quantization produces no consistent safety trend.
We release all prompts, evaluation scripts, and model outputs to support
reproducibility and future lower-bound benchmarking research.

\subsection{Scoring Artefact Corrections}

Two scoring artefacts were discovered during result auditing. First,
\texttt{llama.cpp} generations for SQuAD often included stop tokens, prompt
echoes, or trailing explanatory text after the answer. Exact-match scoring
therefore underreported span accuracy. The corrected scorer strips known EOS
tokens and truncates to the first non-empty output line before applying standard
SQuAD normalization. Second, HaluEval generations sometimes repeated the
instruction text after the answer; because the instruction contains both
``yes'' and ``no'', a full-text parser marked many outputs as ambiguous. The
corrected parser again uses the first non-empty line.

These corrections are conservative: they do not search the entire response for
a favorable answer, and they do not change the gold labels. They only remove
post-answer text that would not be shown as the answer in a deployed QA or
classification interface.

\subsection{Safety Classifier Caveats}

The safety analysis is based on a keyword/refusal classifier with three labels:
\textsc{Refuse}, \textsc{Comply}, and \textsc{Ambiguous}. The current draft
reports refusal rates rather than attack success rates because ambiguous
responses are common in small edge models. For unsafe prompts, a mean refusal
rate of 6.5\% (max 35.4\% across model--setting pairs) means the large majority
of unsafe requests are either complied with or ambiguous. On safe control
prompts, over-refusal is low on average but unstable across pairs (mean 3.5\%,
max 32.8\%), confirming that a few configurations swing toward over-refusal while
most under-refuse. To check the classifier, we drew a stratified 120-response
sample (balanced across refuse/comply/ambiguous strata) and re-judged it with
GPT-4o. Agreement is 78\% (Cohen $\kappa=0.78$): of the disagreements, GPT-4o
converts a few keyword-\textsc{Ambiguous} responses to \textsc{Comply} and finds
marginally more refusals overall, so the keyword classifier does not understate
unsafe compliance. Because the validation sample is stratified, its label
proportions are not the population base rates; the 6.5\% refusal figure comes
from the full unsafe set. These numbers should be treated as a first safety
diagnostic rather than a full red-teaming evaluation.

\subsection{Failure Case Taxonomy}

During result auditing, we grouped failures into four recurring types. This
taxonomy is useful for interpreting PEEL because not all low scores imply
the same kind of weakness.

This taxonomy guided the interpretation of the main results. SQuAD P3 is mainly
a task-contract failure; NQ-Open at E2--E4 combines knowledge limits with
runtime-interface failure; HaluEval's initial low scores were primarily a parser
and runtime-interface issue; and safety variation reflects policy instability
rather than a monotonic quantization effect.

\subsection{Why MT-Bench Is Not in the 4-Tier Heatmap}

MT-Bench is a classic eight-category, open-ended, judge-scored benchmark. Its
unit of evaluation is an 80-question conversation set rather than a 200-example
reference-scored sample. We therefore do not mix it into the P0/P1/P2/P3
reference-task heatmaps. Instead, we use MT-Bench to test a different question:
whether informal prompting changes open-ended instruction-following quality and
whether model-family rankings are stable under deployment conversion. The main
paper reports setting-level and family-level P0/P3 deltas; category-level radar
plots are included in the released analysis notebook.

\subsection{Interpreting Missing Cells}

Missing cells in PEEL are not silently imputed. They arise from two
sources: (1) tokenizer or chat-template incompatibilities that
prevent reliable generation for a task/setting; and (2) optional baseline models
with partial coverage. We report aggregates over available cells and avoid
claiming a comparison when the matched model--setting--prompt set is incomplete.
For prompt-sensitivity claims, we use matched P0/P3 pairs wherever possible.

\subsection{Reproducibility Notes}

The released repository contains three layers of artefacts: raw generations,
per-run score files, and aggregate CSV summaries. The intended workflow is:
(1) run generation jobs with a model key, setting key, dataset key, and prompt
tier; (2) score raw outputs with the task-specific evaluator; (3) aggregate
per-run metrics into \texttt{results/summary.csv}; and (4) derive paper tables
and figures from the summary plus MT-Bench \texttt{scores.jsonl} files. This
separation is deliberate. It allows parser fixes, such as the SQuAD EOS
correction and HaluEval first-line correction, to be applied without rerunning
model inference.

For a new model, the minimal experiment matrix is P0/P3 for SQuAD v2,
TruthfulQA-MC1, NQ-Open, and MT-Bench under E0 plus the intended edge setting.
The full PEEL matrix then expands to all four prompt tiers for
reference-scored tasks and all five settings for deployment analysis. We
recommend running smoke tests with a small example limit before launching the
full 200-example runs, because runtime failures are common in the edge setting
and should be discovered before queueing large jobs.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}

\paragraph{Prompt realism.}
P1--P3 are hand-authored diagnostic prompts. They represent plausible levels of
prompt specificity, but they are not drawn from a user study. The claims should
therefore be read as robustness-to-controlled-prompt-degradation claims, not as
population-level claims about all novice users.

\paragraph{Hardware realism.}
E2--E4 are CPU-only GGUF deployments with constrained thread and memory settings.
They approximate a local edge environment, but they do not cover phones,
NPUs, mobile GPUs, or vendor-specific acceleration. We removed phone-device
comparisons from the current paper because no phone measurements were collected.

\paragraph{Judge reliability.}
MT-Bench relies on GPT-4o-mini as an automatic judge. The judge is useful for
relative instruction-following comparisons, but it introduces model-specific
judgment noise. For this reason, MT-Bench conclusions are reported separately
from reference-scored QA and classification tasks.

\paragraph{Safety scope.}
The safety suite measures refusal/compliance patterns with a keyword classifier.
It is appropriate for detecting deployment instability, but insufficient for
policy compliance claims. A future version should include human annotation or a
stronger policy judge.
